How It's Going

Key terms: A CNA (CVE Numbering Authority) is an organization authorized to assign CVE IDs. GHSA refers to GitHub Security Advisories, published under the CNA shortName "GitHub_M." The February forecast refers to FIRST.org's Average Case projection published February 11, 2026.

Through the first four months of 2026, actual CVE publications are tracking 6,420 CVEs above the February Average Case forecast, representing a 46.3% cumulative overshoot (MAPE: 30.6%). The monthly divergence has accelerated — from +28% in January to +62% in March — before stabilizing somewhat at +59% in April. The February forecast assumed patterns consistent with 2024's growth trajectory, but 2026 is outpacing even 2025's record-breaking pace.

The primary driver is a structural expansion of CNA operations at two organizations. GitHub Security Advisories (GHSA) has grown from 785 CVEs in Jan–Apr 2025 to 4,313 in Jan–Apr 2026 — a 449% increase — now publishing more CVEs than any other CNA. This growth reflects GitHub's expanded curation team and a campaign to backfill CVE IDs for advisories that previously existed only as GHSA identifiers. Separately, VulnCheck — operating as a CNA of Last Resort that assigns IDs to vulnerabilities no other CNA has claimed — scaled from 26 CVEs to 837 (+3,119%), absorbing backlog that previously went unassigned. Together these two CNAs account for the majority of year-over-year growth.

Notably, while administrative cataloging (GHSA) remains the primary driver of volume growth, AI-automated vulnerability research has started moving the needle on major vendor disclosure volumes — most visibly in the Anthropic + Mozilla collaboration (Project Glasswing), which contributed 28 confirmed Firefox CVEs in Q1 2026. AI-discovered CVEs remain less than 1% of total volume but represent a qualitatively new discovery channel.

Measurement note: This analysis uses datePublished from CVE records, which reflects when the CVE was published to cvelistV5 — not necessarily when the vulnerability was discovered or disclosed. Some of the observed growth, particularly from GHSA and VulnCheck, includes backfill of previously-known vulnerabilities now receiving CVE IDs for the first time. The true rate of newly discovered vulnerabilities is growing more slowly than these publication counts suggest.

The GHSA Effect: GHSA's +3,528 CVEs alone account for more than half of the total year-over-year growth. This is a cataloging expansion — increased visibility into existing open-source vulnerabilities — not evidence that open-source software suddenly became less secure. The growth is expected to plateau as the backfill of existing GHSA-only advisories completes.
The AI Discovery Effect: Mozilla's 141% year-over-year increase is an early indicator of frontier AI models acting as defensive security tools. Under Project Glasswing — a joint Mozilla–Anthropic initiative applying large language models to systematic codebase auditing — Anthropic's models autonomously discovered 22 vulnerabilities patched in Firefox 148, and a newer model variant identified 271 legacy bugs and vulnerabilities patched in Firefox 150 (April 2026). VulnCheck has tracked 40 CVEs explicitly credited to Anthropic researchers so far in 2026, with 28 belonging to Firefox. To be clear: AI-discovered CVEs account for less than 1% of total 2026 volume to date and do not materially affect the forecast overshoot. However, the trajectory suggests AI-assisted auditing will become a structurally significant discovery channel in H2 2026 and beyond.

Historical Context — Annual CVE Growth

Trajectory: If the revised projection holds, 2026 will be the fastest growth year since the CNA program expanded in 2024. The CVE ecosystem crossed 1,000 published CVEs/week for the first time in March 2026 and hasn't dropped below that pace since. However, much of this growth reflects improved cataloging coverage rather than a proportional increase in exploitable risk.

Summary & Implications

The February 2026 forecast no longer reflects current CNA dynamics and requires revision. It was calibrated against 2024–2025 behavior that has since been superseded by two structural shifts in how CVEs are assigned:

  1. GitHub Security Advisories (GHSA) expanded its curation team and launched a backfill campaign to assign CVE IDs to existing GHSA-only records. This is a semi-curated editorial process — not fully automated — that went from modest contributor (785 Jan–Apr 2025) to the ecosystem's largest single publisher (4,313 Jan–Apr 2026).
  2. VulnCheck's CNA of Last Resort role filled a gap left by MITRE's reduced capacity, absorbing previously-unassigned vulnerabilities into the CVE catalog at scale (26 → 837).

This is not a "vulnerability explosion" in the traditional sense. The underlying rate of software defects has not increased 46% in four months. Rather, the CVE catalog is expanding its coverage — particularly for open-source library vulnerabilities that previously went untracked or existed only in platform-specific databases. Whether this represents the CVE system "catching up" to reality or a dilution of signal quality is a matter of ongoing debate within the CNA community.

For vulnerability management teams: Plan for 5,800–6,200 new CVEs per month for the remainder of 2026, but recognize that raw volume alone is a poor proxy for risk. Prioritization should lean heavily on exploitability signals (EPSS scores, CISA KEV membership) and affected-product relevance rather than CVE count. Teams relying on NVD-enriched data should monitor whether NVD analysis throughput is keeping pace with this publication volume — enrichment lag creates blind spots. Consider filtering by CNA source when triaging: a GHSA advisory for a transitive npm dependency carries different operational urgency than a Chrome or Linux kernel CVE. Volume growth does not proportionally increase exploitable-and-in-your-environment risk — the vast majority of newly cataloged CVEs are low-to-medium severity library issues that do not warrant SLA-driven remediation.
